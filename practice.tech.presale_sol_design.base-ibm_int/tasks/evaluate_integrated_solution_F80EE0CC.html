Task: Evaluate Integrated Solution
This task conducts a formal review of the entire technical solution before to ensure that it is ready to be proposed to the client.
Disciplines: Quality Management
Purpose

This task is a formal review of the entire solution before it is proposed to the client.  The review focuses on the entire technical solution and is led by someone independent of the team that performed the solution design.  Output from this review may be used by a Proposal Baseline Assessment which will also take into considerations the non technical aspects of the solution. 

The Integrated Technical Review, and its predecessor Technical and Delivery Assessments (formerly, Solution Assurance Reviews in some IBM LOBs), identify strengths and weaknesses of the current solution in meeting user needs.  The review(s) also assess the likelihood that the client will agree to the solution and if it will be successful in meeting the client's success criteria. 

Based on the results of this task, it may be appropriate to iterate back through prior tasks.

      Main Description

      A formal review of the solution is usually required to identify/document implementation issues and recommendations. A user-based walk-through is often performed, that is, the architect takes one or more Use Cases to guide the discussion of the solution as documented in the Architectural Overview Diagram, Component Model and Operational Model.  The team provides observations and subjective feedback to the architect.

      The solution review should evaluate the following aspects of the solution within the guiding principles and constraints originally defined:

      • Availability
      • Capacity and Scalability
      • Performance
      • Backup and Recovery
      • Security
      • Skills
      • Integrity
      • Systems Management
      • Standards and Architecture

      An Integrated Technology Review might be used to provide this solution review if the IBM Services content and Business Transaction Type (BTT) rating warrants it.  The ITR is used to conduct an integrated review focused on the technical and delivery elements of a multi-brand or cross-geo solution.  The ITR ensures that cross-LOB solutions have an end-to-end quality review. 

      Input to the ITR will be the standardized outputs of prior Technical and Delivery Assessments (formerly called a Solution Assurance Review in some IBM LOBs).

      See Guidelines for a further explanation of the ITR process and useful links to additional information. 

      Key Considerations

      If a Technical Delivery Assessment (TDA) or Integrated Technical Review is required or appropriate, the Technical Solution Architect will present the project (Project Definition), key decisions made (Architectural Decisions) and the most important risks, assumptions, issues and dependencies (Viability Assessment).  Additional required work products provide an understanding of the requirements, that is, the functional and quantitative aspects of our solution.  So, at least one of the following are required:  Use Case Model, Requirements Matrix, or Non-Functional Requirements.  Note that Requirements Traceability and Verification Matrix work product could be added by a LOB/Service Line as a fourth option to describe requirements. These work products were chosen to be mandatory starting in 2010 because they would normally be used on large, complex opportunities when TeamSD is used.  That is, they should result in no new or additional work.

      Although not on the "mandatory" list, other work products will be needed to provide a context for the review and to describe the solution being proposed.  The work products reviewed are likely to include:

      • System Context: shows the context of the proposed system to users and other existing systems that make up the solution. 
      • Architecture Overview: describes the overall solution and is often used to explain it.
      • Component Model: shows the relationship and interaction of application or technical components for an important subset of the solution.
      • Operational Model: describes many aspects of the proposal that satisfy the Non-Functional Requirements and reflects the Architectural Decisions that have been made.
      • Estimation Report: captures the estimates used to determine costs and, especially when services are involved, the project schedule.   (Some LOB's also require Estimation Report for TDA and ITR in 2010.) 


      This same guidance applies for other reviews by peers or clients.
