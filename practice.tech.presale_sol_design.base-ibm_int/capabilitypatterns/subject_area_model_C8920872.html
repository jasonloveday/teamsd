Work Product (Artifact): Subject Area Model (APP 408)
The Subject Area Model work product is a graphical as well as textual document of the major groupings of entities that are needed to support an enterprise, a capability or an application.
Purpose

The main purpose of a Subject Area Model Work Product is to:

  • Convey, graphically or textually, the scope of an enterprise, a desired capability or application from the point of the view of the data or information required to support the enterprise, application or capability.
  • Provide parameters and boundaries for subsequent data analysis and data design activities. These boundaries may also impact subsequent architectural decisions.
  • Provide high-level planning constructs with respect to the data / information needs for the capability, application or enterprise.
  • To enable and support a proper alignment between the key information requirements of the enterprise, capability or application with the goals and objectives of the same.
  • To enable consultants and senior management to explore the constraints on the business and opportunities for, and implications of change.
  • To provide a useful vehicle for communication of the information and to encourage client ownership, acknowledgement and acceptance of the information presented.
  • Develop a document around a set of information that represents the consensus built by customer stakeholders.
  • Provide input into a detailed release plan and project estimates for a subsequent development effort.

This work product also represents a hand-off point between various IBM competency groups. For example, it is hoped and anticipated that an Industry Solution Unit (ISU) or Customer Relationship Management (CRM) practice might work with a customer to develop their core value propositions, capability models, and capability enablers. At some point, however, in the identification of capability enablers, there may be a realization that one or more of the enablers suggests a Business Intelligence (BI) (or Electronic Document Management (EDM) or E-business) solution. If the Subject Area Model work product is within the scope of the consulting engagement, it is appropriate to engage a qualified competency SME (i.e. BI practitioner, or EDM or E-business SME) to join the team. If not, the Subject Area Model work product represents the starting point for a consulting engagement in which a BI (or EDM or E-business) competent practitioner would document the information areas specific to enabling the BI (or EDM or E-business) value proposition.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users):  In a pre-sale context, the primary purpose of this artifact is to identify required subject areas, related data items, candidate data sources and additional solution requirements that will result from the analysis of these data sources. Understanding of subject areas will be helpful in defining the responsibilities of components.

Main Description
The Subject Area Model may alternatively be referred to as a Conceptual Data Model or Business Information Model. A Subject Area Model work product is deliberately high-level in definition as well as use. As such, it is an excellent communication vehicle for an executive or business management audience. This work product is generally developed in parallel to work products that document a high-level vision of a solution. While other work products focus on the processes, organization or technology which needs to be developed or enhanced or purchased, the Subject Area Model work product focuses on the data which must be present in order to enable an enterprise, a capability or an application.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users):  In a pre-sale context, the primary focus of this artifact is the identification and description of candidate data sources and the identification of additional data and information requirements based on the fit/gap of the candidate data sources to the intended solution.

Key Considerations

The development of the Subject Area Model work product depends greatly on two key skills, 1) industry/functional subject matter expertise and 2) facilitation experience. For example, a customer whose primary product is women’s apparel, whose main distribution method is through catalog sales, and whose business intelligence interest is centered around controlling costs and increasing the average purchase quantity and total dollar amount per order, would need an industry subject matter expert on the project team who is familiar with the main drivers of cost control and customer buying habits in this environment. Facilitation skills are important because it is a primary technique used to engage the customer in articulating their information needs and the decisions the desired information needs to support.

A third, but less important skill dependency for the development of this work product is data analysis or data modeling skill.

A significant and essential by-product of the development of this work product is the consensus built around the results. By inviting input from all stakeholders with an interest in the enablement of a defined capability, and by leading them all through a structured and impartial process for the rational identification of related subject areas, the consultant achieves the ‘buy-in’ to the scope of the application or capability.

Depending on the level of detail available as input into the activities to develop this work product, the project team may have to iterate several times through steps 3 and 4 of the development approach outlined above.

This work product is typically developed as a graphical representation of the subject area groupings. Sometimes known relationships between groupings of information are noted. Textual information is typically a description of each subject area.

The Subject Area Model work product is generally packaged into a major consulting engagement deliverable by combining its content with the results of other work products. Because it does not make sense to discuss or develop subject area information without a target application or capability in mind, this work product cannot stand on its own without this other information.

The Subject Area Model work product requires a subsequent analysis and design effort to justify its benefits. If the client appears unable, unwilling or unlikely to proceed beyond a capability definition engagement, this work product may be postponed.

A critical by-product of this work product is the client consensus built around the scope of the application or capability.

This work product is more fully developed as a logical data model and physical data model in subsequent iterations of the requirements definition process.

This work product should be developed at the appropriate level of granularity for its scope and purpose. For example, if the scope of the information analysis is at the enterprise level, then the subject area model should reflect an enterprise view.

The scope of this work product should validated as early in the development cycel as possible, especially as it relates to the identified stakeholder groups whose input will for the basis of the results.  Make sure that the client stakeholder groups and client executive management agree to the results documented in the work product and can articulate the value of the process used to develop the results.

Verify the client understand the purpose of and why the subject are model needs to be produced and the client understands:

  • What it represents, how it was developed and its importance in guiding subsequent development work
  • That it is perceived as a 'guiding' document to be referred to and developed more fully in subsequent iterations of the requirements definition process
  • The risk inherent in not developing this work product
  • The limts of the subject area model and that its main purpose is to develop a general application or capability scope
  • The skills required to develop this work product in the event they wish to assume ownership of its maintenance in the future.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users): For a discussion on the necessity of this work product and appropriate levels of elaboration for a pre-sale environment, consult the guidance entitled Pre-sale Work Product Usage for Team Solution Design.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

This work product demonstrates our understanding of the structure and content of the customer’s information requirements at a high level. Should this work product not be prepared, then any combination of the following may occur:

  • There is no basis for partitioning the organization’s data and scoping subsequent projects.
  • There is a danger of missing important information needs and its implications.
  • Some key functional requirements related to missing subject area groupings may not be identified.
  • An assessment of information sharing requirements, across business units or functional areas, is not possible.
  • An incomplete picture of an organization’s (or capability’s or application’s) needs may result in erroneous recommendations regarding the development of some solution areas.
  • There is no foundation to develop a coherent database strategy.
  • There is no assurance that business goals and objectives will be properly supported.
  • The accuracy of the estimation of subsequent projects may be affected.
  • The project team begins an Analysis and Design effort in which the scope and vision of the project have not been established. This situation jeopardizes both requirements definition as well as project estimation activities.
  • There is no communication document to refer to in the event of turnover in customer management or the project team.
Reasons for not needing

Under certain project circumstances, this work product may not be needed, or it may need to be prepared in an abbreviated form. These circumstances are presented below.

A Subject Area Model work product may not be needed in circumstances where:

  • The client organization or another vendor or another IBM consulting organization has formulated this information and can provide it in documented form.
  • Client management has mandated that the scope of an engagement specifically omit this work product. However, the relative risk of release plans and project estimates created without benefit of the Subject Area Model work product will increase.

This work product should not be optional when documenting and developing a candidate list of project initiatives. It may be postponed from one phase to a later phase but should not be skipped.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users): This artifact may not be required in situations where the intended solution has very limited data requirements or where the client assumes complete responsibility for data sources. In the latter case, it may still be important to understand what additional requirements the intended data sources imply.
Representation Options
The Subject Area Model is typically notated graphically and descriptively.
It should be generally clear from the name of the subject area what information is encompassed.
Textual information generally adds additional descriptive information as well as clarifying or pointing out a nuance of the model.
