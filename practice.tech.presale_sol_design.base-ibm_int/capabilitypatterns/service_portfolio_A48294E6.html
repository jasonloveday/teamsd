Work Product (Artifact): Service Portfolio (ART 0583)
This artifact captures the list of candidate services. It is first elaboration of the Service Model and forms the basis for capturing the dependencies and the detailed specification. The list of candidate services is organized and categorized in a hierarchy to better manage the porfolio.
Purpose

To document and manage the collection of services in scope as they are refined through the various stages of Service Identification.

Relationships
Container Artifact
  • Service Model
Description
Main Description

The Service Portfolio captures information about the collection of services in scope as a whole, evolving through multiple iterations of service identification and refinement. Initially, we use the Service Portfolio to document the list of candidate services identified during service discovery activities. As functional areas are identified, we refine the Service Portfolio by categorizing the candidate services according to functional relationships into a service hierarchy. Later, we use the Service Portfolio to capture the list of services to be exposed as determined by service litmus tests, as well as the scope of exposure (e.g.: external, organization, business unit, or department) for those services.

There is only a single instance of this artifact for a Service Model showing all services.

Brief Outline

This artifact consists of:



Section Description
Service Portfolio List of candidate services discovered during service identification activities
Service Hierarchy Candidate services organized using a business significant categorization scheme, making evaluation of candidate service more manageable. Domain decomposition typically provides the classification scheme through functional area analysis
Service Exposure Documents the rationale behind why we chose to expose a given candidate service or group of services in the service hierarchy or composition. The service litmus test is applied to candidate services to make exposure decisions.




Notation

Service Portfolio


For simplicity the Service Portfolio can initially be represented as a list of candidate services as shown in Figure 1.

 

Figure 1: Service Portfolio - initial service list

As the number of candidate services increases, an unstructured list can quickly become unmanageable. Therefore, as soon as possible a service classification scheme should be identified so that candidate services can be organized into groups within the classification hierarchy. The Service Hierarchy organizes services in the service portfolio using a uniform classification scheme. The classification scheme is often based on the functional areas identified during business domain decomposition. Notation for the Service Hierarchy is an outline format as shown in the example in Figure 2.

 

Figure 2: Service Portfolio organized using a classification hierarchy

While a simple list of service names can be a quick starting point, it will eventually be important to capture additional information about each service. This information can be subdivided into two types: information that supports service identification, and information that supports service specification.


Service identification is focused on building a portfolio of services that can be associated with business functions, business goals, assets such as existing systems, and an indication of whether the service is considered a candidate service, or has been chosen for exposure. The table shown in Table 2: Service Portfolio template is a template that can be used to document services at the level of detail needed in the service portfolio.

Table 2 Service Portfolio Template

Service: name of service
Service Operation: name of service operation
Description: short description of service or service operation
Service Owner: owner of the service e.g. could be Business or IT owner
Associations:
Business Process: name of (or reference to) business process that the service is associated with (this typically aligns Process Definition). Depending on the level of detail reached in analysis activities this could be a business process, or a process step from some level of process decomposition. More specificity in the reference is generally better.
Business Component/Business Function: name of business component/business function that the service is associated with (this typically aligns with Functional Areas). Depending on the level of detail reached in analysis activities, this could be a business domain, a functional area, a specific function. More specificity in the reference is generally better.
Goal: name of a goal or sub-goal that the service is associated with (this typically matches a goal or sub-goal listed in the Goal-Service Model).
Asset: an asset that is associated with the realization of the service (this typically matches a system listed in the Business Function / System Matrix)
Service Disposition: “C” for candidate service, “E” for a service chosen for exposure, “NE” for a service not chosen for exposure, “F” for a service chosen for exposure and funded
Status: Specified for services that have been specified but not implemented, Implemented for services that have been specified but not deployed, Deployed for services that have been deployed but not approved by SOA Governance, Managed for the services that are governed, Retired for service that have been deprecated.
Service Re-factoring and Rationalization
Not all candidate services meet the criteria needed to be chosen for exposure. Service Exposure decisions can be made by applying Service Litmus Tests. The template shown below can be used to capture service exposure decisions and the rationale for making the decision.

Table 3: Service Exposure Decision Template

Service: name of service
Service Operation: name of service operation
Business Alignment: does the service meet business alignment SLT criteria
Composability: does the service meet composability SLT criteria
Redundancy Elimination: does the service meet redundancy elimination SLT criteria
Client Defined: litmus tests to satisfy specific business needs for client
Exposure Decision: Y for expose, N for don’t expose
Exposure Comments: cases may arise were a decision is made by the business to expose a service even if it does not pass all SLT tests. Document the reason for overriding the SLT test results.
Exposure Scope: whether the service is exposed to Project, Domain, Line of Business, Enterprise, Eco-system.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout this artifact it would be difficult to properly define and categorize services and to make exposure decisions. This could lead to gaps in the service portfolio, proliferation of unnecessary services, and inconsistencies in how services were exposed.
Reasons for not needingThis artifact is not needed if we do not need to externalize service descriptions at the edge of a significant organizational boundary (i.e. at the edge of a major line of business within an enterprise, or at the edge of the enterprise).
