Work Product (Artifact): Viability Assessment (ART 0530)
This artifact is an analysis of how viable the proposed system is and provides a measure of how likely it is that the system will satisfy critical requirements.
Purpose
This artifact is created as a result of performing a Viability Assessment. Its purpose is to record the findings of that assessment, the risks associated with the findings and the recommendations made to mitigate against those risks.

Description
Main Description

This artifact documents the findings, risks and recommendations which are the results of performing a Viability Assessment. A finding (and the associated risks) could be anything that is likely to impact the delivery of the solution such as:

  • The ability to meet the solutions requirements.
  • The availability of the right skills.
  • The stakeholder acceptance of the technical elements of the proposed solution.
  • The ability to meet the schedule and budgetary constraints defined for the solution.
  • Lack of reusable elements in the solution.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users): Besides capturing project risks, the Team Solution Design version of viability assessment documents issues, assumptions and dependencies.  In a pre-sale environment, it is rarely necessary to maintain a separate issue log although a work product is available in UMF and might be needed during implementation.  By documenting risks, assumptions, issues and dependencies in one place (also known as the RAID log), we can see them all at once. Also, it is important to capture the assumptions and dependencies that might impact the proposal, implementation and delivery.
                    
Very early in Team Solution Design, we begin capturing risks, assumptions, issues and dependencies in the Viability Assessment artifact.  This may start by identifying potential problems (based on observations), which are analyzed to determine findings (statements of fact), which then lead to an understanding of the potential risks (impacts on the proposal, contract or project).  The first time we conduct a formal viability assessment, we update this initial version of the artifact. Further elaboration of the viability assessment is performed when it is refined and again when we evaluate the integrated solution. Frankly, this work product is updated throughout the life of the project, including during implementation and deployment.

It is particularly helpful if the descriptions field (Risk Description, Assumption Description, etc.) refers back to the source of the functional or non-functional requirements or architectural decisions that generated the item.  For example, a risk item documenting difficult to achieve response times should refer back to the non-functional requirement (including its ID number) that stated these response time requirements. Risks are usually the most serious and must be resolved or mitigated.  Issues are usually transitory in nature and can be delegated to others for resolution.  Assumptions are often made and dependencies discovered that must be documented to properly assess the viability of the project. Architectural decisions are also often based on these assumptions and dependencies. Since the basic viability assessment artifact does not include assumptions, issues and dependencies, they may need to be explained to the deployment architect during transition to delivery.

In the early stages of solution design, tables such as the RAID log (see associated template) may be sufficient to capture risks, assumptions, issues and dependencies as they are discovered and dealt with. The associated viability assessment template is intended to record these risks, assumptions, issues and dependencies. Later on, a formal peer review is probably appropriate.  The Viability Assessment provides a list of risks as input to the GS Risk tool when it is used for these later peer reviews.  Assumptions, issues and dependencies may need to be maintained in the Viability Assessment or they need to be restated as risks since GS Risk only handles risks.

Depending on the risk and complexity of the project, there are several formal peer reviews that might be required to identify likely risks. Under Supporting Materials, you'll find a presentation with speaker notes that describes quality practice reviews: Technical Delivery Assessment (consolidated with Solution Assurance), Integrated Technical Review, Proposal Baseline Assessment and Project Management Review.  These reviews are held depending on revenue, complexity and other criteria from the IBM Brand leading the proposal to the client.

Key Considerations

Consider the following when performing a Viability Assessment:

  • A Viability Assessment can be performed either as part of a formal Quality Assurance review or on a more "ad-hoc" basis when stakeholders or project management have raised concerns outside the normal review cycle.
  • Use any available checklists as a starter set of questions to ask.  Modify these before the review to suit the needs of the particular project.
  • Ensure viability issues are clearly stated and there is an action plan in place to address them before presenting the final report.
  • Ensure people who will be interviewed are briefed beforehand as to the purpose of the assessment so that they can prepare the right material.
  • When interviewing people as part of an assessment try to use neutral language as much as possible, do not use language which could be construed as apportioning blame.
  • It is better to raise critical issues early, when they can be resolved, rather than blindly following a schedule that leads the project team down the wrong path.
  • The assessment needs to balance risks against costs, remaining sensitive to the broader issues of project success.
  • Viability Assessments can be difficult and sensitive.  The reviewer needs to be a persuasive communicator who can raise and discuss sensitive issues.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users): This artifact provides a way to document the status of the project.  Together with the project definition, it is the primary vehicle for communication with clients and teammates. The associated checklist, is not intended for use by Team Solution Design users.

For a discussion on the necessity of this work product and appropriate levels of elaboration for a pre-sale environment, consult the guidance entitled Pre-sale Work Product Usage for Team Solution Design.  

Tailoring
Impact of not havingNot having this work product increases the risk of a system being late, incomplete, or over budget.
Reasons for not needing

This artifact is typically not needed for projects with a low risk of viability issues, such as:

  • Small projects
  • Projects using an established infrastructure
  • Projects using a widely accepted technical solution
Representation OptionsThe Viability Assessment can be delivered in presentation format or as a formal report.
