Task: Define Key Services
This task identifies those key Services, whether from within the enterprise or from an external service provider, which integrated in the Solution Design.
Purpose

The task, Define Key Services, is use to:

  • Identify the key Services (whether Application, Platform and Infrastructure) which will be used by the Solution and must be integrated into the Solution Design. The focus is on both existing services (whether from the enterprise or from a service provider) and on the identification of new services.
  • Assess how these key Services can address important functional and non-functional requirements.

Main Description

Service Oriented Architecture (SOA) is an architectural style that has become increasingly important for the design of business solutions. SOA requires a service provider, service requestor and typically some form of mediation. The architectural principles of SOA (encapsulation, loose coupling, reuse and composability) are well established while the programming model has continued to evolve.

There is a rapidly increasing set of SOA Services (typically REST-based) that are available in the marketplace for Cloud-based services - whether for Infrastructure, Platform or Application level. The adoption of Cloud technology has rapidly accelerated the availability of Service APIs from Service Providers and is based on SOA principles. In this task, where SOA Services are referenced, they refer to both Enterprise-based (internal) SOA Services and those Services from a Cloud-based Service Provider.

The consideration of SaaS based SOA services should be indicated in the Candidate Asset List which is an input to this task. For more information on SaaS considerations, consult the White Paper on SaaS and TeamSD

As described in the Guideline: Service Oriented Architecture Design for Team Solution Design, there are many client projects that involve SOA but don't require the identification and realization of services. In other cases, considerable effort will be required to identify existing and candidate services. To account for these differences, the steps required in this task will vary based on the following considerations.

  • Are any new SOA services required?
  • Is the pre-sale engagement IT or business driven?
  • If new SOA services are required who will be responsible for the design and realization?

Based on the answer to these questions, IBM’s post-sale role may range from infrastructure provider to leading the design and realization of new SOA services. Most situations can be represented by one of the following three scenarios. By selecting the scenario that most closely applies, you will be able to select the most appropriate steps in this task.

Scenario 1 - Likely proposal: IT driven / additional SOA infrastructure only / no new SOA services
In scenario 1, the expected outcome is a proposal for additional SOA infrastructure to support an existing SOA environment. Creation of new services is not anticipated and IBM SOA design services will not be required. A scenario 1 opportunity is typically driven by the client’s IT organization. If the requirements for the new SOA infrastructure can be developed without knowledge of existing services, it will not be necessary to execute any of the steps in this task. In this case, simply refer to associated Guideline: Service Oriented Architecture Design for Team Solution Design for guidance on how Team Solution Design tasks and work products work together in an SOA solution. In other cases, you may need to discover SOA services already existing in the client's SOA infrastructure that are to be reused. Basic information about the relevant services will be captured to enable design of the additional SOA infrastructure. The purpose of identifying these existing services would be to serve as a basis for determining infrastructure requirements and to support illustration and explanation of the proposed architecture.

Scenario 2 - Likely proposal: IT driven / new SOA infrastructure / new SOA services / client responsible for SOA services design and realization
In scenario 2, the expected outcome is a proposal for new or additional SOA infrastructure with new SOA services to be designed and implemented by the client, perhaps with assistance from others such as IBM SWG Services or a non-IBM services provider. A scenario 2 opportunity is typically driven by the client’s IT organization. As a Technical Solution Architect, in this task, you will need to identify new SOA services as part of the pre-sale activity. Similar to scenario 1, the purpose of identifying these new services would be to serve as a basis for determining infrastructure requirements and to support illustration and explanation of the proposed architecture. In addition these new candidate services could serve as a basis for a pre-sale demonstration or proof of concept.

This scenario results in identification of a limited set of services with limited elaboration and realization. The level of detail required will vary, with more detail required to support a custom demonstration or proof of concept. Consequently, it is important that if the client will be responsible for post-sale SOA services design and realization efforts, the client also has sole responsibility for estimation of the design and realization effort.

Refer to the associated Guideline: Service Oriented Architecture Design for Team Solution Design for additional guidance on how Team Solution Design tasks and work products work together in an SOA solution.

Scenario 3 - Likely proposal: Business driven / new SOA infrastructure / new SOA services / IBM responsible for SOA services design and realization
In scenario 3, the expected outcome is a proposal for additional SOA infrastructure with IBM responsible for the design and implementation of new SOA. In this scenario, the Technical Solution Architect will typically request assistance from an SOA SME rather than execute the steps in this task. Consider the following capabilities for use in this scenario.

  • Cloud Advisory services for insight on available services relevant to the client’s industry and specific initiative.
  • The Service Oriented Modeling and Architecture (SOMA) method provides in-depth guidance on how to move from the business models created through CBM or similar business analysis techniques, to the models required by a SOA solution design. A SOMA workshop (sometimes referred to as "SOMA-lite") can be used to begin this process. This workshop might be used pre-sale or, more likely, to kick off a SOMA services engagement.

In scenario 3, it is very important that a SOA SME be involved in the pre-sale activity if the proposal is expected to contain details or estimates of the post-sale design activity. Even though a SOA SME may lead the SOA services aspect of the solution the Technical Solution Architect may still use Team Solution Design to develop other aspects of the SOA solution (for example, the SOA infrastructure and architecture). In this case, refer to associated Guideline: Service Oriented Architecture Design for Team Solution Design for guidance on how Team Solution Design tasks and work products work together in an SOA solution.

Steps
Assess Opportunity Scenario

The objective of this step is to determine which if any of the following steps apply. This is done by answering a few basic questions and selecting one of the three scenarios described above. These include:

  • Are any new SOA services required?
  • Is the pre-sale engagement IT or business driven?
  • If new SOA services are required who will be responsible for the design and realization?

Note that scenario 3 is a special case in which no further steps in this task are required.
Identify Relevant Services

The objective of this required step is to identify and categorize the set of existing (scenario 1) and or a few new (scenario 2) services that are within scope for the solution being proposed.  The service identification step ensures that a representative set of services are captured and organized based on function or importance to the organization.  There are three techniques available to identify these existing or potential new services:

  1. Domain Decomposition: a top-down business-driven decomposition of a business domain, from the business aspect to IT aspect as shown in the figure below. Note that when a Component Business Model exists, CBM competencies can be treated as domains. 

  2. Existing Asset Analysis: a bottom-up analysis that examines assets such as existing custom, packaged applications, and industry models, to determine what can be leveraged to realize service functionality.  These existing assets might already contain existing services or might help us identify new services.
  3. Goal-Service Modeling: an analysis that starts with Business goals that are decomposed into sub-goals and supporting services. Key performance indicators and associated metrics are identified to measure the attainment of sub-goals through the services identified.  Goals, sub-goals, and key performance indicators may have already been captured in Project Definition. 


The relevant services identified will be captured in the Services Portfolio section of the Service Model work product.  Refer to the Service Model Template for Team Solution Design.   
Establish Services Hierarchy
The objective of this optional step is to describe the existing or new services using a uniform classification schema that is often based on the analysis in the Identify Relevant Services step.  This will probably be most useful in analyzing existing assets since we are unlikely to identify a large number of new services in a pre-sales context. 

The services hierarchy will be captured in the Service Portfolio section of the Service Model work product.  Refer to the Service Model Template for Team Solution Design.
Determine Services Dependencies

The objective of this optional step is to describe the relationships between services that arise in the larger context of how they will be used.  The types of dependencies are:

  1. Functional Dependencies: the service is composed of multiple other services that provide the function.
  2. Temporal Dependencies: there is some pre or post condition or processing requirement that will need to be performed by other services.

These dependencies will be captured in the Service Dependencies section of the Service Model work product.  Refer to the Service Model Template for Team Solution Design.

Document Service Composition and Flow
The objective of this optional step is to describe the composition and flow of short running, non-interruptible processes (micro-flow) and long running, interruptible processes (macro-flow). 

This information will be captured in the Service Composition and Flow section of the Service Model work product.  Refer to the Service Model Template for Team Solution Design
Key Considerations

For the use of external Services, from an SaaS or other provider, be sure to consult and comply with the client's Security Guidelines for the use of such services, including the protection of data in transit and data residency.

Even though a SOMA engagement might not be proposed, it is useful to understand some of the essentials of SOMA. The Whitepaper: Understanding SOMA Essentials for Team Solution Design was created for this purpose. As described in this whitepaper, SOMA activities are grouped into three major steps: Identification, Specification and Realization Decisions. In the full SOMA method, these major steps are accomplished through multiple tasks which create various sections of the Service Model. In Team Solution Design, you will use a single task with multiple steps. The resulting work product will create a subset of sections required by the full work product. Each section will typically also be less elaborate than required in a post-sale engagement. The necessary number of sections and level of elaboration will depend on several factors, including which of the above scenarios is appropriate and the complexity of the expected proposal.

One final note for pre-sale solution designers (Team Solution Design users): As mentioned above, occasionally, we will develop one or two services as part of a Proof of Concept.  In these cases, we will limit our PoC scope (and investment) to only what is required to prove our capability.  For example, we might "stub out" parts of the service (like access to backend applications or data sources) that are already well known.  For this reason, these services created for a PoC are rarely usable in production without further development.
