Guideline: Developing the Requirements Matrix
Guidance on the development of inputs to the Requirements Matrix artifact, a primary input to the Solution Design.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Identify and Outline Requirements
  • Requirements Matrix
Main Description

The development of inputs to the Requirements Matrix is begun during the Understand Client’s Business and Needs phase by the client and solution design team.  As part of the Identify and Outline Requirements Task team members refine the inputs into a set of requirements to the point where a solution design can be developed, based on those requirements, with reasonable confidence that the design can be built to meet both the requirements and the client’s business need.  The results are documented in Requirements Matrix artifact.

Prepare the Requirements Matrix according to the following steps:

  • Review the existing artifacts to determine if they contain functional requirements or system constraints that the solution must meet.  Documents to review can include:
    • Standards
    • Technical Environment
    • Business Direction
    • Opportunity Plan
    • Project Definition
    • System Context
    • Use-Case Model
  • Identify business functionality requirements. Include the following:
    • What the solution does
      • Business functionality
      • Management functionality
      • Audit functionality
      • Administrative functionality
      • Workflow
    • What the solution produces
      • Screens and reports
        • Queries
        • Instrumentation
        • Logging
        • Data retention
      • Messages and information for other systems
      • Physical effects
    • How the solution operates
      • Requirements from Use Cases / Activity sequences / Epics
      • Roles and Actors: Who (or what) can do what actions
      • States and Modes: Operational vs. test or maintenance modes
      • Interfaces: Physical, functional, and security
  • Identify general requirements that will be needed by the client for their environment and resources.  Include the following:
  • Deployment (method of deployment, timing, continuity of operations)
  • Special environments (Development, test, maintenance environments)
  • Client preparation
    • Business Process Changes
    • Training
    • Data center / facility modifications
    • Data migration
  • Run, Operate, Maintenance, and help requirements
    • Software, database, and environment maintenance and control
    • Ongoing security management
    • Help Desk Support
    • Documentation
  • Validate final requirements with client team members in a work session or workshop.
  • Assemble and refine the Requirements Matrix. 

Definitions:

Administrative Functionality: Refers to the controls to be built into the system to modify its operation and behavior as well as more typical activities such as adding or removing users, groups, and permissions. These are also referred to as systems management requirements.

Audit Functionality: Refers to features incorporated into the system to support audits required by business rules and/or government (or industry) regulations.  This is typically logging certain transactions or activities within the system.

Business Functionality: Not to be confused with business level requirements in a hierarchy, this describes the business functionality of the solution element being specified. Ex: “The Post_Interest module shall calculate and post the daily interest accrued to the account using equation (2) specified below”

Management Functionality: This refers to (generally autonomous) controlling aspects of the execution of the system, such as controlling the timing and execution of certain business functions.

Workflow Functionality: Refers to the automation aspects of a sequential business process being implemented by the system.
