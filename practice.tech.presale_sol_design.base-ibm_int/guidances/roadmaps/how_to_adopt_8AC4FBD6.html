Roadmap: How to Adopt the Pre_sale Solution Design Practice
This roadmap provides guidance on how to adopt the Pre-sale Solution Design practice
Main Description

Getting Started

In order to successfully adopt this practice, it will be necessary to obtain certain planning information. This may already exist or be acquired through the adoption of another practice which must precede this one. The information required might typically be represented in UMF work products such as Standards, Business Direction, Current Organization Description, Project Definition, Team IBM Account Plan, Account Profile and Opportunity Plan. An example of the types of activity necessary to produce the prerequisite information may be seen in the Plan Phase of the Team Solution Design Delivery Process.

It is also helpful to assess the size and complexity of the opportunity and the likely solution design as well as the client's expected buying behavior. This will help you determine if all off the practice will be needed or if the practice will need to be augmented with additional content. Guidance on formally establishing the client's Buying Behavior and Business Transaction Type (BTT) may be found in IBM's Client Value Method (CVM).

Based on the size and complexity determine what roles will be required. As written, the majority of the practice is intended for the role of Technical Solution Architect. In some cases significant detail regarding such areas as SOA, estimation or service delivery approach may require additional SME roles.

Common Pitfalls

Use only the aspects of the practice that are required based on the client's buying behavior and the business transaction type. In some cases the simplicity of the proposed solution is such that only a few work products are required.

For large or complex opportunities, always consider what additional tasks and work products might be required. The practice contains guidance in how to adopt additional method content for those situations.

Be flexible during use of the practice. In a pre-sale context, it is often not possible to understand the scope, complexity or features of the solution at the outset. It may sometimes be necessary to adopt additional method content in the midst of the pre-sale engagement.

Although the practice indicates an expected precedence of tasks, a typical pre-sale engagement does not always afford the gathering of information and creation of work products in such a linear way. Expect to be iterative as necessary. It is also expected that level of elaboration of work products may vary as indicated by the nature of the solution.
