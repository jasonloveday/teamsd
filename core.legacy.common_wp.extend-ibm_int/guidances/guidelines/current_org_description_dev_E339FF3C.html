Main Description

1. Identify information sources needed for in-scope areas of the organization and obtain access

3. Understand the behavioral elements of the organization through interviews and research
4. Understand the enabling elements of the organization through interviews and research

Identify information sources needed for in-scope areas of the organization and obtain access

This development approach can cover all elements of the organization, but you should ensure that you select and focus only on those elements that are in scope for the engagement. If a Change Project Description Form (ODR Inc.) has been completed for this engagement, it should help to clarify scope.
A variety of materials can be used to understand the current organization, including:

  • Organization reports, annual reports, mission statements, and policy documents
  • Interviews with managers and executives
  • Interviews with other employees
  • Functional (Finance, HR, IT) information sources for job descriptions and descriptions of management systems and programs
  • Interviews with client team for informal practices.

Understand the structural elements of the organization through interviews and research

Get an understanding of the in-scope structural elements at the enterprise, team/function, and individual levels by using the tool "Checklist to Investigate the Structure of the Current Organization." It addresses:

  • The structural design of the organization at the firm or enterprise level (e.g., key organization units, policies and operating principles, key extended-enterprise relationships)
  • The structure of the organizational units themselves (e.g., functional groups and cross-functional teams, missions for the units, formal relationships among units)
  • The design of individual jobs for both management and employees (e.g., job roles and accountabilities, team roles, process used for job design)

Two theoretical models are available, which can provide ways to characterize the organization's structure.

  1. Bureaucracies as enabling vs. coercive; Structure/Other, "Organization Structure - Class Presentation" (see pages 7-8)
  2. Organization types presented in a comparative view; Overview/Other, “FOAD Toolkit Phase 5” - FOADT10.pre, “IT Organization Design Guidelines,” pages 13-17

Understand the behavioral elements of the design through interviews and research

Get an understanding of the in-scope behavioral elements on the enterprise, team/unit, and individual levels by using the Checklist to Investigate Behaviors in the Current Organization. This is aimed at understanding:

  • The dominant culture of the organizational unit(s) at which the design activity is aimed (e.g., statement of culture/values at the corporate level, stated vs. actual corporate culture),
  • The dominant behaviors of formal units and teams and informal groups, (e.g., informal networks, formal communication flows, learning and mentoring practices, conflict management, teaming behaviors)
  • How individuals are expected to and actually behave (e.g., dominant behaviors and attitudes vs. desired norm on task management, communication, role modeling, coaching)

Consider using a culture assessment questionnaire (licensed by Professor Daniel Denison and available through the IBM Organization Change Knowledge Network) as a method to characterize the organization in terms of values, culture, and attitudes. This is a 50-question survey that describes the current culture in five areas and highlights strengths and weaknesses in each. However, remember that the purpose of this work product is to describe the organization rather than evaluate it, and be aware that the Denison tool uses value-laden language. The assessment of the current organization is described as a separate work product.

Understand the enabling elements of the organization through interviews and research

Get an understanding of the in-scope elements of the organization, which enable (i.e., support and motivate) the structure and behaviors of the organization by using the Checklist to Investigate the Enablers of the Current Organization. This is aimed at understanding:

  • The nature and extent of enablers that are in place both at the top level across the organizational unit(s) and within them (e.g., HR strategies, communications infrastructure and programs, organizational performance analyses and measurement, core competency management, knowledge management, change management)
  • How teams or units are supported, keeping in mind that different units may deliver different data and conclusions (e.g., team learning methods, work group performance support systems, IT support, performance analyses and management systems, competency management)
  • What enablers are in place to support and motivate individuals (e.g., individual performance management methods, learning methods and programs, job/role/task/competency definitions)
