Guideline: Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix
The Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix technique maps major groupings of entities against potential solution area development projects. This matrix is deliberately high-level in definition as well as use. As such, it is an excellent communication vehicle for an executive or business management audience.

Main Description

This matrix is generally developed in parallel with other work products, which document a potential set of solutions and the data needed by the solution set. It ties a specific set of information groupings to a specific solution area.

Purpose

The main purpose of a Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix is to:

  • Convey, in tabular or graphical format, the scope of a desired application or capability by linking the application and its data dependency.
  • Demonstrate the commonality of need between solution areas for the same information.
  • Demonstrate the commonality of need between subject areas for the same functions.
  • Provide parameters and boundaries for subsequent data analysis and data design activities. These boundaries may also impact subsequent architectural decisions.
  • Provide high-level planning constructs with respect to the data / information needs for the capability, application or enterprise.
  • To enable and support a proper alignment between the key information requirements of the enterprise, capability or application with the goals and objectives of the same.
  • To enable consultants and senior management to explore the constraints on the business and opportunities for, and implications of change.
  • To provide a useful vehicle for communication of the information and to encourage client ownership, acknowledgement and acceptance of the information presented.
  • Develop a document around a set of information that represents the consensus built by customer stakeholders.
  • Provide input into a detailed release plan and project estimates for a subsequent development effort.

The construct of a mapping between solution areas and subject areas is simply a mechanism for the consultant to visually and powerfully convey their understanding of the relationship between solution areas and subject areas.

Contexts of Use

This technique assists the consultant in justifying the prioritization of solution development projects by highlighting areas of risk. For example, if the proposed solution area includes a subject area whose information does not exist or is known to be of poor quality, the consultant would be justified in recommending a lower priority for that solution area.

Step 1: Perform initial information gathering and and analysis preparation

Verify that the client understands the purpose of the Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix – what it represents; how it was developed; and its importance in guiding the subsequent development work.

Validate the scope of the matrix, especially as it relates to the identified stakeholder groups whose input will form the basis of the results. Determine the solution areas and subject areas under consideration

Step 2: Assess extent of capability definition, solution area and subject area definition

Specifically, for each of the Solution Areas under consideration:

  • Identify impacted organizations from a consumer, supplier, governance, and support perspective.
  • Identity level of granularity of the information is required.
  • Determine degree of fit with existing capabilities.
  • Determine the degree of overlap or dependencies between areas.

For the Subject Areas under consideration:

  • Identify the candidate sources for the information, both internal and external.
  • Identify the levels of abstraction available for each Subject Area, in detail and timing.
  • Identify known risks to information quality, especially correctness and completeness.

You may choose to base these findings on existing documentation, interviews or facilitated workshops, or a combination of these techniques.

Step 3: Conduct interviews with business and technical communities

Validate with client stakeholder groups and client executive management the solution areas, information requirements, and their level of involvement with the solution areas, and stated business and technical impacts for implementing or deferring a solution area.

Verify that the client also understands the Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix is a ‘guiding’ document to be referred to and developed more fully in subsequent iterations of the requirement definition process. Its main purpose is to develop a general application or capability scope, pointing out areas of commonality between solution areas or subject areas.

Step 4: Review, analyze and document findings

The matrix is typically developed as a tabular mapping of the subject area groupings to the solution areas. Known characteristics of the mapping are indicated.

For example, each cell of the table might indicate:

  • Whether data is primarily Created, Read, Updated or Deleted. The use of this technique results in a matrix that is sometimes referred to as a CRUD matrix.
  • Whether data is available through existing capabilities, or is under development or obtainable from external sources.
  • What stakeholders groups use, own, govern, or support the data.

A significant and essential by-product of the development of this matrix is the consensus built around the results. By inviting input from all stakeholders with an interest in the enablement of a defined capability, and by leading them all through a structured and impartial process for the rational mapping of subject areas to solution areas, the consultant achieves the ‘buy-in’ to the scope of the application or capability.

Tools

This matrix can be developed with any number of office productivity tools, word processors, spreadsheets, graphical applications such as Freelance, or using desktop database software, such as MS Access. The choice of tool should consider the skill set of the developer, the scope and depth of the matrix being undertaken, the office productivity tools available to the receiving audience, and the output format of the engagement’s deliverables.

Validation and Verification

Validate that client stakeholder groups and client executive management agree to the results documented in the matrix and can articulate the value of the process used to develop the results.

Verify that the client understands what a Solution Area/Subject Area Model Matrix is and the risk inherent in not developing this work product.

Verify that the client also understands the limits of a Solution Area/Subject Area Model and that it’s main purpose is to develop a general application or capability scope, pointing out areas of commonality between solution areas or subject areas.

Advice and Guidance

This technique demonstrates an understanding of the linkage of a customer’s high-level information requirements and the enterprise, capability or application needs being enabled. The Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix requires a subsequent analysis and design effort to justify its benefits. If the client appears unable, unwilling or unlikely to proceed beyond a capability definition engagement, this technique may be postponed.

Should this matrix not be prepared, then any combination of the following may occur:

  • Changes in data or information requirements related to changes in scope of a solution area are not easily demonstrated or documented.
  • Likewise, changes in a set of solution areas that result in additional or enhanced subject areas are not easily demonstrated or documented.
  • Commonality of need (data as well as function) is not easily identified. An assessment of information sharing requirements, across business units or functional areas, is not possible.
  • The project team begins an Analysis and Design effort in which the scope and vision of the project have not been fully established. This situation jeopardizes both requirements definition as well as project estimation activities.
  • Under certain project circumstances, this matrix may not be needed, or it may need to be prepared in an abbreviated form. These circumstances are presented below, where:
  • The client organization or another vendor or another IBM consulting organization has formulated this information and can provide it in documented form.
  • Client management has mandated that this specific technique be omitted from the scope of an engagement. However, the relative risk of release plans and project estimates created without benefit of the Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix will increase.
  • Verify that the client understands the key skills required developing this matrix in the event that they wish to assume ownership of its maintenance in the future.
  • The development of the Solution Areas/Subject Area Model Matrix depends greatly on two key skills: 1.) Industry/functional subject matter expertise; and, 2.) Facilitation experience.

For example a customer whose:

  • primary product is women’s apparel
  • main distribution method is through catalog sales
  • business intelligence interest is centered around controlling costs
  • increasing the average purchase quantity and total dollar amount per order

The customer would need an industry subject matter expert on the project team who is familiar with the main drivers of cost control and customer buying habits in this environment. Facilitation skills are important because it is a primary technique used to engage the customer in articulating their information needs and the decisions the desired information needs to support.

  • A third, but less important skill dependency for the development of this matrix is data analysis or data modeling skill.

Estimating Considerations

A major estimating factor is the degree of definition of the input work products. The more complete and more consensus developed regarding their completeness, the less time it will take to complete the matrix.

If it is determined that more definition needs to be developed for the input work products, a second estimating factor is determining the method to be used in soliciting additional requirements and confirming the results. The major methods of information gathering used are:

  • Documentation review
  • Interviews (individual or group)
  • Facilitated group sessions
  • A combination of interviews and facilitated sessions

The selection of which method to use is dependent on factors such as availability of documentation, the number of individuals to be interviewed, the culture of information sharing in the organization, the time limitations of the project, and the relative difficulty of coordinating calendars for group sessions.

Once the method of information gathering has been determined, the important estimating factor is the number and length of information gathering and verification sessions to conduct. In addition, adequate time to prepare and summarize each session should also be set aside.

In general, the larger the number of stakeholders to interview and the greater the outside demands on their time, the greater the impact this entire work effort will have on project duration.
