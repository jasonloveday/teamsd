Artifact: Client Imperative (ART 0696)
The Client Imperative describes what the client is trying to achieve, the strategy for achieving it and the expected business benefits.
Purpose
The Client Imperative clarifies the opportunity description and provides a basis for breaking down a large, abstract or complex opportunity into distinct transformation activities.

Description
Main Description

The Client Imperative is typically used in a pre-sale context to formally describe what the client is trying to achieve and why. The following is an example of a client imperative definition.

"Enable a <xyz bank's> Multichannel Delivery Strategy with an Optimal Architecture and Technology Platform"

As described in the brief outline, a complete imperative definition contains information that answers key questions such as:

  1. What is the client trying to achieve? (the imperative)
    The client imperative definition is a succinct and actionable statement that expresses an answer to this question. The imperative is also accompanied by further explanation or description. The initial client imperative is typically expressed by the client sponsor. Completing all aspects of the imperative is accomplished by analysis of client information gathered in interviews and meetings. These statements may include statements of fact, opinion, problems, issues, need and intent. As the imperative is inferred from this analysis, its correctness and viability should tested by answering the following additional questions.

    If this opportunity originated with the client's interest in one of the IBM Blueprint's Industry Imperatives, the Client Imperative represents a specialization of the Industry Imperative, tailored to suit a specific client's needs. In such cases, information associated with the Industry Imperative may be helpful in documenting the client imperative.

  2. Why is the client trying to achieve this? (motivation, the compelling reason to act)
    Compelling reasons to act may include implementing a disruptive competitive strategy, external environmental factors or concerns about performance and efficiency. Essentially, this question is about establishing the client's motivation which serves as evidence that the statement "The client is trying to achieve (imperative definition)" is true. Given an initial imperative that has been expressed directly by the client, proposed by IBM or inferred from your analysis, the evidence from interviews, meetings and other statements by the client should support your conclusion about what the client is trying to achieve. For example, an interviewee may directly contradict the initial imperative definition by saying "that might be interesting, but what we really need to do is the following". Evidence of this sort may indicate that the initial imperative definition needs revision or specialization, or that more than one imperative should be considered. If a client statement expresses a particular need or problem, evaluate whether the imperative under consideration satisfies that need or addresses the stated problem. Would achieving the stated imperative yield the desired business outcomes and benefits as stated in client interviews? If the evidence does not support the proposed imperative, it may be necessary to revise the imperative (with the client sponsor's approval).

    In cases where the client imperative is related to an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult the Industry Imperative documentation for information on common industry motivation for addressing this imperative.

  3. How can the client achieve this? (the solution strategy)
    If the defined imperative is realistic, it should be possible to express the response to the imperative as a high level solution strategy. This strategy would describe an approach, including tactics, in high level business and architectural terms, possibly based on a successful approach used elsewhere. Evidence of a viable solution strategy should further support the belief that the client imperative has been well defined and is achievable. Strategy aspects (tactics) may range from establishing new business capabilities to specific capability enablers.

    In cases where the client imperative is related to an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult the Industry Imperative documentation for possible stages of transformation and available industry solutions that may inform the solution strategy.

  4. What are the likely inhibitors?
    This is a final test of the expected viability of the proposed imperative. As desirable as achieving the results may be, inhibitors such as high cost, limited skills and resources, cultural and societal issues, etc. may force a reevaluation of the imperative.

  5. What are the expected business outcomes and benefits?
    Although normally intended for solution proposal, guidance on developing a value proposition may provide insight for this section. If the client imperative represents a specialization of an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult information associated with the Industry Imperative for candidate business outcomes.

A correctly defined imperative will ensure that the imperative is supported by strong evidence of the client's need and intent and that there is a proposed solution strategy that illustrates a viable approach to addressing this imperative.

Key Considerations

The following comments describe a few of the many situations that may arise when attempting to define a client imperative.

  • The “given” imperative: In some cases, the client may carefully consider all of the issues and express a well formed imperative in initial discussions. This becomes the candidate imperative that is used during interviews and analysis. Although the imperative may stand up to further analysis, it needs to be tested with the other questions above. In some cases it may need revision (with the client’s agreement) if, for example, it is too general (not actionable) as discovered when developing a solution strategy.

    If the "given" imperative is based on one of the IBM Blueprint's Industry Imperatives, it will likely have to be specialized to suit a specific the client's specific needs.  
     
  • The “implied” imperative: The client sponsor may express only issues and problems where the implied imperative is simply “fix this”. Although this information is a helpful starting point, it will be necessary to infer the imperative from the results of interviews, analysis and potential solution strategies.
     
  • The “too narrow” imperative: If the imperative is expressed by the client initially, it may be very specific, narrow or low level. Accepting this definition may unduly constrain the proposed solution strategy and omit other desirable elements of the strategy. This type of imperative may be improved by asking questions that lead to a more general definition of the imperative, which addresses not only the original imperative, but other vital issues that should have been included.
     
  • The “too broad” imperative: Some imperatives that relate to general business transformation may be too broad or abstract to be actionable. In such a case, it may be helpful to decompose the imperative into several more specific, actionable imperatives that are supported by analysis of user statements and together satisfy the intent of the original imperative. The solution strategy of a broad, complex imperative is likely to have multiple significant aspects (referred to as tactics). These aspects (tactics) may indicate that the imperative could be refactored into individual imperatives. Ultimately the granularity of the imperative is an arbitrary choice, influenced by the need for clarity and actionable individual imperatives.

Enterprise Architecture Influence: Existing enterprise architecture is an important influence on solution design. See the associated Guideline: The Role of Enterprise Architecture in Solution Design for guidance on EA artifacts (or their equivalents) that should be considered when developing this work product.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Assuming that the opportunity is large, complex and likely to consist of multiple significant aspects, the impact of not using this work product could result in any of the following.

  • The resulting solution design may overlook significant aspects of the solution which would be necessary to achieve the client's intent.
  • Implementation of the resulting solution may be unnecessarily complex because it has not been factored into a program of separately manageable projects.

Reasons for not needing

This work product is not needed if the following conditions exist.

  • The opportunity is well defined and is considered to be of modest scope and complexity.
  • The identify opportunity task is sufficient for clarifying the opportunity.
  • The opportunity is likely to result in a single solution design and implementation project.
