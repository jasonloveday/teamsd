Guideline: Defining the Client Imperative
This guideline offers general advice on how to define the client imperative.

Main Description

Defining the Client Imperative is accomplished through analysis of client statements and ultimately focuses on a single statement of the imperative and accompanying explanation that expresses what the client is trying to achieve. Determining the imperative is not always straightforward. To ensure that you arrive at the correct imperative(s) and therefore an appropriate opportunity description, it is necessary to answer several key questions.

  1. What is the client trying to achieve? (the imperative)
    The client imperative is a succinct and actionable statement that expresses an answer to this question. The imperative is also accompanied by further explanation or description. Defining the imperative is accomplished by analysis of client information gathered in interviews and meetings. These statements may include statements of fact, opinion, problems, issues, need and intent. As the imperative is inferred from this analysis, its correctness and viability should tested by answering the following additional questions.

    If the "given" imperative is based on one of the IBM Blueprint's Industry Imperatives, it will likely have to be specialized to suit a specific the client's specific needs.  

     
  2. Why is the client trying to achieve this? (the compelling reason to act)
    Essentially, this question is about establishing the client's motivation which serves as evidence that the statement “The client is trying to achieve [imperative statement]” is true. Given an initial imperative that has been expressed directly by the client or inferred from your analysis, the evidence from interviews, meetings and other statements by the client should support your conclusion about what the client is trying to achieve. For example, an interviewee may directly contradict the candidate imperative statement by saying “that might be interesting, but what we really need to do is the following”. Evidence of this sort may indicate that the candidate imperative needs revision or that more than one imperative should be considered. If a client statement expresses a particular need or problem, evaluate whether the imperative under consideration satisfies that need or addresses the stated problem. Would achieving the stated imperative yield the desired business benefits as stated in client interviews? If the evidence does not support the proposed imperative, it may be necessary to revise the imperative.

    In cases where the client imperative is related to an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult the Industry Imperative documentation for information on common industry motivation for addressing this imperative. 
     
  3. How can the client achieve this? (the solution strategy)
    If the defined imperative is realistic, it should be possible to express the response to the imperative as a high level solution strategy. This strategy would describe an approach, including tactics, in high level business and architectural terms, possibly based on a successful approach used elsewhere. Evidence of a viable solution strategy should further support the belief that the client imperative has been well defined and is achievable.

    In cases where the client imperative is related to an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult the Industry Imperative documentation for possible stages of transformation and available industry solutions that may inform the solution strategy. 
     
  4. What are the likely inhibitors?
    This is a final test of the expected viability of the proposed imperative. As desirable as achieving the results may be, inhibitors such as high cost, limited skills and resources, etc. may force a reevaluation of the imperative.

  5. What are the expected business outcomes and benefits?
    Although normally intended for solution proposal, guidance on developing a value proposition may provide insight for this section. If the client imperative represents a specialization of an IBM Blueprint Industry Imperative, consult information associated with the Industry Imperative for candidate business outcomes.

There following comments describe a few of the many situations that may arise when attempting to define a client imperative.

  • The “given” imperative: In some cases, the client may carefully consider all of the issues and express a well formed imperative in initial discussions. This becomes the initial imperative that is used during interviews and analysis. Although the imperative may stand up to further analysis, it needs to be tested with the other questions above. In some cases it may need revision (with the client’s agreement) if, for example, it is too general (not actionable) as discovered when developing a solution strategy.
     
  • The “implied” imperative: The client sponsor may express only issues and problems where the implied imperative is simply “fix this”. Although this information is a helpful starting point, it will be necessary to infer the imperative from the results of interviews, analysis and potential solution strategies.
     
  • The “too narrow” imperative: If the imperative is expressed by the client initially, it may be very specific, narrow or low level. Accepting this definition may unduly constrain the proposed solution strategy and omit other desirable elements of the strategy. This type of imperative may be improved by asking questions that lead to a more general definition of the imperative, which addresses not only the original imperative, but other vital issues that should have been included.
     
  • The “too broad” imperative: Some imperatives that relate to general business transformation may be too broad or abstract to be actionable. In such a case, it may be helpful to decompose the imperative into several more specific, actionable imperatives that are supported by analysis of user statements and together satisfy the intent of the original imperative. The solution strategy of a broad, complex imperative is likely to have multiple significant aspects (referred to as tactics). These aspects (tactics) may indicate that the imperative could be refactored into individual imperatives. Ultimately the granularity of the imperative is an arbitrary choice, influenced by the need for clarity and actionable individual imperatives.
