Main Description

In a pre-sale context,  IBM may propose design and implementation services and in that case will be expected to provide estimates of time and costs. When estimating is required and is non-trivial, it is important to involve experts who are well qualified to provide such estimates. The following illustration offers insight as to the various roles that may be helpful or necessary in the estimating process. 

 

Note that there are two different SME estimating roles, one for development of estimates and one for independent review.

When the required estimation is non-trivial, it is important to follow good practices. The following are 10 best practices that have been identified by the Global IBM Estimating Community:

  • Quantify the things you are dealing with
  • Use more than one estimating approach
  • Use actual historical data and metrics
  • Base your project control measurements on the same metrics used for developing the estimate
  • Use a recommended estimating tool when you can
  • Understand your input and output data
  • Estimates should always be carried out by a practitioner with relevant experience
  • Always document the assumptions made
  • A second set of eyes is a good thing â€“ i.e. get a peer review !
  • The later you make the estimate, the more you'll know, and the more accurate it will be -> estimate more than once !

Additional information on estimating can be found at the web site below.

The following is an introduction to the estimating web site:

Estimating is an exercise in taking a set of requirements at a certain level of detail, understanding how those requirements are intended to be met using a given solution, and applying an estimating model. An estimate, properly prepared within the limitations of the model, is by itself neither a good estimate nor a bad estimate. The estimate reflects the influences of both requirements and solution. A bad estimate occurs when the facts have been interpreted incorrectly, gaps are not identified and judgment calls are proven to be erroneous. - Cheryl den Hartog, IBM Canada
