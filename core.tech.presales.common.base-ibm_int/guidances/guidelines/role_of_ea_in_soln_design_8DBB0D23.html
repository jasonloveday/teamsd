Main Description

Introduction
There are numerous definitions of enterprise architecture and its practice and certainly little need to create yet another. The following definition of the EA discipline was created by experts in the IBM EA community.

“The EA discipline defines and maintains the architecture models, governance and transition initiatives needed to effectively co-ordinate semi-autonomous groups towards common business and/or IT goals.”

This definition is useful in the sense that this guideline focuses on what is created in the practice of enterprise architecture and how it should influence solution design. The following quote from IBM’s EA educational material captures this idea succinctly.

“Enterprise Architecture constrains the individual project, for the benefit of the Enterprise”

Although not a perfect analogy, enterprise architecture is often compared to the concepts embodied in city planning. A city plan identifies the common infrastructure needed (streets, utilities, sports and cultural facilities, playgrounds, shops, banks, municipal services, etc.) and ensures they are made available. City planning also typically involves zoning restrictions that ensure that individual homes can be built in a compatible environment and have convenient access to streets and utilities. Some communities have protective covenants that go even further by constraining choices in size, building materials and even color. The quote above says it well. With Enterprise architecture, as with city planning, the enterprise does benefit, but while the individual project or solution design may be constrained, it also benefits by having access to the “utility aspects” and common infrastructure of the enterprise architecture that simplify development and integration of a new solution.

Although enterprise architecture should normally play a role in solution design, the following two cases depict examples (hopefully rare) of a plausible reason not to focus on existing enterprise architecture in solution design.

  • In the first example, the client has explicitly established that the solution will be a completely greenfield effort. This means that for some business or technical reasons, the client believes there is value in creating a solution that has no dependencies on any existing enterprise architecture. Potential reasons may include the need for extremely fast development or an interest in using best of breed technology and design, not previously used within the current enterprise architecture.
  • In the second example, client requirements and specifications are very complete and explicit and constrain the solution design in most important aspects. Although not necessarily true, this situation suggests that in effect the requirements and specifications reflect the existing enterprise architecture. In essence, key architectural decisions have been already made by the client and there is no need to consult their existing enterprise architecture.

In most other cases, enterprise architecture should ideally have a prominent role in solution design. This ensures that both the enterprise and the solution share in benefits such as common infrastructure and that the individual solution fits into the broader strategy in order to achieve common goals.

Using EA in Solution Design.

The remainder of this guideline focuses on how to take advantage of enterprise architecture in solution design. The following illustration depicts the relationship between enterprise architecture and solution design.

The ability to take advantage of enterprise architecture in solution design will vary according to the circumstances at the client. The following are examples of what one might encounter.

  • The client has in place a well documented enterprise architecture, complete with active governance. This may be a rare occurrence, but In this case, the solution design process can derive full benefit from being informed by existing EA work products. This is the ideal case. in which one can use examples from the following section to identify the appropriate EA work product for guidance in solution design.
  • The client has some level of enterprise architecture, but it is not complete or well documented. In this case, there are no documented EA work products to refer to, but it is still important to understand whether there are deliberate aspects of an enterprise architecture that may exist. There is always an enterprise architecture. Whether it is deliberate, elegant or effective is another matter. The suggested approach in this case is to investigate what enterprise architecture may exist by using the appropriate EA artifact to guide your research with the client. It may also be helpful in such cases to record what exists, even though it may be a partial result. This documentation may be helpful in follow-on projects and may even influence the client to more formally document their enterprise architecture.
