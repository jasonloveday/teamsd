Artifact: Standards (ARC 310)
The Standards work product lists and details pre-determined standards for a defined scope of the business and the information technology environment.
Domains: Architecture
Work Product Kinds: Standard
Purpose

The Standards work product is used to:

  • Promote the consistent and effective implementation of corporate standards, policies and architectures and regulations.
  • Guide an organization in meeting its objectives (e.g. Security standards guide an organization in meeting security objectives).
  • Understand the preferences of an organization - alternatives which do not conform to these standards can either be eliminated from consideration, or the work required to allow a deviation from, or expansion of, the specific standards can be undertaken in a timely manner. This is particularly relevant in organizations where packaged solutions are preferred.
  • Document any pre-determined components that must be catered for in the future Enterprise Architecture (with its constituent business, applications, data, accessibility and technology architectures).

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users): Our purpose is limited to an understanding of Standards that are applicable to the projects we are involved in. As a planning work product, Standards is continually updated as new information becomes available.

Description
Main Description

Standards provide the general “policy” level statements that are implementation-oriented and provide an auditable exception process. These sets of “rules” may make specific mention of technologies, methodologies, implementation procedures and other detail factors of the policy. A standard can be defined as something with a pre-described specification, that is measurable, recognized as having authoritative value, and which an organization chooses to implement as a basis for 'good practice'.

These standards may have been determined by a previous study or may be de-facto or de-jure standards based on installed technology or management practices. The rationale, or source, of such determinations is documented for reference purposes. The work product also documents the implications of deviating from these standards. The deviation process itself will be documented as part of an overall management framework. Any significant components for which there are no established standards are also noted.

Standards can be developed from two viewpoints:

  • From a corporate policy standpoint - in which case the standards will be enterprise-wide and technology independent
  • From an architectural standpoint - in which case the standards will be technology and product specific.

Standards can be defined in many areas; the following ones are typical, dependent on the scope of a specific engagement:

  • Management standards
  • Technology standards
  • Application development standards
  • Security standards
  • Accessibility standards
  • Service delivery standards

Key Considerations
  • This work product needs to be developed as a joint client/consulting team exercise, usually in a workshop. It is vital that all parties understand the issues, contribute to the discussion, and support the agreed position.
  • Accessibility standards should only be developed by someone knowledgeable and skilled in accessibility.
  • Security standards should only be developed by Methodology-trained Security professionals. Additional resources available in the IT Security ICAP Database.
  • The documentation notation itself should conform to the established standards, e.g. accessibility.

Note for pre-sale solution design (Team Solution Design users):   As a planning work product, the Standards work product is continually updated as new information becomes available. 

The client might have well documented standards which are available to you. Standards are often specified in several categories:       

  • Current and de-factor standards (As-is)
  • Future and official standards (To-be)
  • Old but still supported standards (Sunset)
  • Old and no longer supported standards (Retired) 

These standards are sometimes color coded.  For example, as-is = green, to-be = blue, sunset = yellow, retired = red.

For a discussion on the necessity of this work product and appropriate levels of elaboration for a pre-sale environment, consult the guidance entitled Pre-sale Work Product Usage for Team Solution Design. 

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Standards always exist in an organization, whether explicitly or de facto. If they are not documented there are a number of potential impacts for the organization:

  • Potential for costly decisions to be made without regard to standards - subsequent implementations that do not conform may not meet the enterprise level business goals and objectives.
  • Without agreed standards the different parts of the organization may fail to consistently comply with company policies and could result in the significant exposure of corporate assets. This is particularly true of Security and Accessibility standards.
  • The customer may have to renew resources if the solution does not meet the enterprise standards.
  • The enterprise or subgroups within the enterprise may fail to meet regulatory or internal requirements for security or accessibility because there are no available resources to maintain compliance with the standards.
Reasons for not needing
  • A set of current, comprehensive standards already exists - in this instance, it may be appropriate to review how they are used and conformance is managed.
  • Production of standards is outside the scope of the engagement.
