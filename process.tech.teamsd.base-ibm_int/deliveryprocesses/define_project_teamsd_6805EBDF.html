Task: Define Project
This task will define important aspects of the project, including goals, objectives, success criteria, approach and scope.
Purpose
The purpose of this task is to turn an identified and validated opportunity into a defined project.  This includes a brief answer to the question: "What are we doing on this project and why?" This task also has the purpose of establishing project sponsorship, which includes both an Executive Sponsor (who can make the decision to buy) and a Technical/Middle Manager Sponsor (who will be responsible for implementing the solution).

Main Description

The primary output of this task is the project definition but what is learned here might also impact the prior work products.  As the project proceeds, the project definition artifact may refer to other work products and other documents such as contracts.

The point is: the artifact created by this task is updated through the life of the project.

In this task, we will determine project goals, objectives, success criteria, approach, and scope.  A key input for this task is the CVM Opportunity Plan work product. We will also identify the project participants in terms of key sponsors, stakeholders, and the project team.  In addition, a plan for project communications should be developed.  These will be documented in the project definition work product.  Note that we will also document key functions required in the project definition, or, if there are many or they are very complex, we may choose to document them with one of the requirements artifacts.

Mack Hanan, in Consultative Selling, gives this as the Consultant's Credo: "Consultants sell money, not products. They transact returns from investments, not sales. Their price is an investment, not a cost. Their performance is measured by the amount and rate of customer's return, not by product performance benefits. They work inside their customer's businesses as partners, not from the outside as vendors. They relate directly to customer line-of-business managers and business function managers, not purchasing agents. They work at these middle management levels on a long-term, continuing basis, not from bid to bid. Their focus is not on competitive suppliers but on competitive profit making for their customer partners and for themselves."

Although IT Architects spend most of their time on technical issues, they must enable their client to:

  1. add more profits
  2. add profits sooner, and
  3. add profits with greater certainty than without you.

For IT Architects, #3 is often the most important area of focus.

TeamSD was designed to be scalable from very small, simple engagements to large, complex ones.  However, more guidance on how to address very large, complex opportunities is sometimes needed.  Also, sometimes TeamSD may need to be supplemented by additional tasks and work products related to specific service line offerings and associated delivery processes.  The Guideline: Tailoring Team Solution Design Process for Large Complex Engagements was developed to provide further information on how to handle these opportunities.

Alternatives

If you used Design Thinking in the Ideation Task, then you should reflect the stakeholders agreement on the Hills for this initiative in the Project Definition. If the client has provided guidance on the related contract approach - a Discovery project or a project with one or more Hills included, be sure to capture that also.

Capture also the expected Delivery Process or Method that the client is expecting based on discussions following any Ideation workshop.
