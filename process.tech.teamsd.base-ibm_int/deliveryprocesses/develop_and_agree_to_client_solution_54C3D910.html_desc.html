Activity: DEVELOP and Agree to Client Solution
This activity develops a proposal for a solution that addresses the client's requirements and can be delivered by IBM.
DescriptionWork Breakdown StructureTeam AllocationWork Product Usage
Purpose

The purpose of this pre-sale solution design step (step 3 of CVM) is to jointly develop and propose a solution that provides value to both the client and IBM.  Both the client and IBM reach final agreement on how the solution will be implemented.  Starting with the client's value drivers and expectations for the opportunity, the team agrees to provide the promised value in the solution we propose, including implementation, and in the manner in which we engage with the client.  The outcome of this activity includes:

  • Client agreed-to proposal
  • Selected solution
  • Value justification (including cost/benefit analysis, image, etc.)
  • Proof of capability (test evaluation, proof of concept)

The progress indicator for this activity is a final proposal and a signed agreement.  That is, the opportunity is won (or lost).

Description

During this Activity, we will design the solution that will address the requirements identified in the "EXPLORE Options and Approach" Activity. During the process, we will continue to refine our understanding of the requirements and the role played by members of the client team (see Project Description work product).  Occasionally, we will even learn that a fundamental "fact" that we learned during the Explore Activity is so significant that we need to re-evaluate if this opportunity is Qualified.  That is, we may decide that our capabilities can not address their requirements to the level of their expectations.  When this is the case, we must either redefine the project so that the client and IBM can be successful or let the client know that we will not be proposing a solution.

At this point in the client buying process, the buyer may use an evaluation procedure to determine the best option (e.g., RFP, business case, customized demo, proof-of-concept, test evaluation, test for indicators of performance, or other "proof").  An evaluation plan is implemented to select the solution alternative, including the best products. The solution is reviewed to determine what validation is required to ensure success.  Any concerns associated with the decision of a specific option are overcome. 

The following are done during this Activity by technical sales:

  • Design/develop solution, specify components – include IBM or client Delivery Owner.
  • Determine solution performance and impact.
  • Provide input for refined benefits and business case – Value Proposition.
  • Support QA and engage Risk Manager, if appropriate. 
  • Define project tasks, responsibilities, timeline in a high level Project Plan.
  • Define delivery requirements.  Ensure IBM and client delivery resources are assigned and the delivery leaders are in place.
  • Present our proposal, negotiate a contract.

During this Activity, we will take at least two opportunities to involve our peers, that are not directly involved in the project, to take an objective, outside view of our work.  In the first (Task: Survey Candidate Assets and Optimize Solution Approach), we'll assess our initial view of the solution to ensure that we have not repeated a pattern which has caused unsuccessful projects in the past.  One of those common mistakes is to not exploit applicable assets.  Whether we use a formal approach like a Solution Optimization Workshop, or ask the opinion of someone we respect, an outside view can helps us make sure that we are on the right path.  (See Task: Survey Candidate Assets and Optimize Solution Approach.)

The second time (Task: Evaluate Integrated Solution) we take an objective view of the project with the help of our peers will be when we do a formal assessment of the solution we will be proposing.  Strong linkage to the IBM Business Unit quality practices must occur to gain necessary approvals and guidance that the solution is accurate and the risk is acceptable. The Business Unit quality practices will prescribe the type of activities and reviews that must be performed during this Team Solution Design Activity.  For example, we will be using a Solution Assurance Review or Technical Delivery Assessment and Integrated Technical Review when the size, complexity and risk warrant it.  Depending on our relationship with the client, this will often involve a review of critical parts of our solution with key influencers and decision makers (that is, those responsible or accountable).  It's rarely a good idea to have the client "surprised" by our solution.

We can verify our value when the client decision leader / team confirms and agrees to:

  • Expected solution performance, impact and benefit.
  • Project Plan, schedule,  success criteria and resources - who does what.
  • Price and terms.

At the end of this Activity, the opportunity is Won and the transition to implementation can begin.

Objectives Guidance Materials Tips/Considerations
Establish Architecture Overview(s) for ongoing playbacks – The details of the solution need to be communicated with many different stakeholders, e.g. Infrastructure teams, EA practice, Operations, Business Users, etc. A clear vision of solution from each perspective should be created to support communication and validation (replay). The task, Develop Architecture Overview, focuses on conveying to the client the high-level abstraction of the proposed system or application. The diagram(s) will serve as a basis for discussion, explanation and evolution of the architecture. The diagram(s) may also serve as a starting point for a services engagement. The Architecture Overview along with System Context and Architectural Decisions are amongst the most valuable communication assets to stakeholders. We must always be validating our understanding of the target outcome and the shape of the current high-level solution with the client and other key stakeholders through formal or informal playbacks.
Develop High-level “white box” architecture viewpoints – How is the solution going to be built? As applicable consider the Service View, Component View, and Operational Views. Be sure and understand how each logical component supports the overall solution (functional) requirements and also how the proposed placement and topology will support the solution non-functional requirements. Also document any architectural decisions made. Functional Viewpoint may be captured via Component Model and/or Service Model. Detailed guidance for building these models are included in Define Key Services and Develop High Level Component Model.

Operational Viewpoint is captured in an Operational Model with appropriate guidance found in Develop High Level Operational Model.

Architectural Decisions made during solution design should be captured to make sure the what and the why is transparent to all stakeholders. Guidance can be found in Document Architectural Decisions.

The solution details will be elaborated along both the functional and operational viewpoints. During pre-sales, most of the effort will be focus on a limited set of “architecturally significant” aspects of the solution design.
One should make extensive leverage of any available reference architecture(s) to minimize risk and improve quality.
Many architectural decisions are made in response to client standard and other constraints. Accurate documentation of these is significant to prevent rework later in the project.
Transition Solution Design to Proposal – The proposal requires understanding the delivery approach, BOM and solution estimates and with this perform a thorough assessment of integrated solution and proposed project. Guidance on establishing the who, what, and how the solution is to be delivered is provided by Identify Delivery Approach.
Guidance for solution and project estimates can be found in Develop Solution Estimates .
With delivery details, many new issues, assumptions, dependencies and risks need to be assessed. Guidance is provided in Refine Viability Assessment.
Technical reviews either formal or informal need to be conducted prior to the formal proposal. Guidance is provided in Evaluate Integrated Solution.
Final guidance for the proposal is provided in Propose Solution, Resolve Concerns.

Key Considerations

One should be aware of the buyer behavior of the client and consider its impact on the approach and effort taken during the explore and develop activities. The client buyer behaviors are:

  1. Value for Money: Clients want a specific technology or service from a trusted brand configured and delivered to their specifications on time at competitive prices, simply and efficiently.
  2. Trusted Supplier: Clients want innovative, best in class technology and support services, integrated with their existing infrastructure, delivered with high-touch project management from one client-focused team from the sale through delivery fulfillment.
  3. Innovation Partner: Clients want a customized approach for a complex project with high strategic business impact and risk from business-oriented thought leaders and industry experts with a deep relationship-based understanding of the client.
