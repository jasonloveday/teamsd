Activity: EXPLORE Options and Approach
This activity defines a client/IBM project, identifies the client's requirements and early decisions with rationale.
DescriptionWork Breakdown StructureTeam AllocationWork Product Usage
Purpose
The purpose of the CVM Explore phase is to identify the key client requirements, develop a vision of the solution and assess the probability of a successful project from a client and IBM point of view. The team EXPLORES with the client the options that offer what the client wants and values and recommends the best. The team demonstrates IBM unique value, showing how we go beyond the competition to offer the value the client wants. The progress indicator for this activity is a qualified opportunity.

Description

From an outcome perspective, this Activity has the goal of ending with a qualified opportunity where we have identified a project which is well aligned with IBM’s ability to deliver and will provide quality value to the client. The process, which is collaborative with the client, involves identifying the scope and high-level approach of a candidate project based on a clear understanding of a set of requirements derived to satisfy a set of business objectives.

For such a project, IBM and the client should have a clear vision of the solution -- details will be defined in the DEVELOP and Agree to Client Solution activity. Is this project, as defined by our knowledge of the requirements and our initial vision of the solution, going to be successful for the client and IBM? If our best assessment is "yes", then the opportunity is "qualified" and we proceed with further solution design. If not, we need to either work with the client to redefine the requirements and Project Description (scope, timeframe, functional and non-functional requirements) or we should decline to participate in the project. It is this honest assessment of our capabilities versus the client's expectations that will yield the most successful projects for the client and IBM. This is of particular importance if the Solution Design activity is in response to a specific solicitation such as a RFP. In other opportunity environments, this activity may be very iterative as the Technical Solution Architect works to settle on a project that can be successful for both the client and IBM.

It is during this Activity that most of the requirements are understood and the vision of the solution is formulated. The approach taken to define the solution is also decided. Most of the work products created during this Activity will be further refined during later Activities. The level of detail needed is just enough to show the client we understand their needs. During the next Activity DEVELOP and Agree to Client Solution), we will show the client we are able to address their requirements with the solution we've designed.

At this point in the client buying process, IBM and the client may start with an array of choices and begin to narrow them down to the best option. At other times, more preliminary work is needed to explore and establish as set of possible options, this is the role of ideation. During Explore consider the impact from the use of optimal IBM resources, such as IBM Client Centers, to provide differentiation and additional subject matter industry and technical expertise.

Refer to the activity objectives below, but the following are done during this Activity by technical sales.

  • Assess the opportunity and reconfirm the Buyer Behavior for this Opportunity. Modify our approach to meet each individual buyer’s expectations.
  • Identify desired IT capabilities and impact; show how IBM options uniquely meet these needs.
  • Develop benefits and technical components of the business case.
  • Confirm the target solution outcome and approach with the client via some form of playback such as demonstrations.
  • Identify technical resources and investment requirements.
  • Identify risks and potential issues.
  • Qualify our pursuit with IBM and align resources.

We can verify our value when the client sponsor:

  • Confirms that we understand their desired business/IT capabilities, impact and benefits.
  • Confirms the IBM preferred approach that meets their desired needs.
  • Acknowledges IBM Unique Value and expertise of the team.

Objectives Guidance Materials Tips/Considerations
Establish Project Scope – Clarify the target business outcome and what business value will be delivered by the Solution Design. From an IT perspective, what are the boundaries of the Solution Design and where is it positioned within the Enterprise? There are two tasks within this activity that separately address project scope. The first, Define Project, focuses on the stakeholders, who they are and what KPIs will determine project success for them. It also starts to consider project timeline and cost expectations. The second,Describe System Context, identifies what systems and users will interact with the proposed solution. Understanding the integration and interface points of solution is critical input to determining the solution approach and candidate technologies.
Capture Critical Requirements – What requirements will the system deliver? It is critical to document what the system will do (Functional) and how it will do it (Non-Functional) - so that all parties are in agreement.

What will the system deliver and how well will those capabilities be delivered? This is the essence of this objective that will be iteratively elaborated over time. It is critical that our understanding of the target goal of the solution is made transparent with extensive playbacks with all parties involved.

The overall plan for the solution should be stated in a series of Hills. These capture a target outcome for a particular Persona with an associate value measure (wow factor). All requirements in some way should map back to and support the intent of the Project Hills!
As the details of the Solution approach are worked out, the discrete functions of the solution should be described and captured. Guidance for this can be found in the task, Identify and Outline Requirements and typically will start with high-level Use Cases that describe the critical system behaviors from the perspective of one or more Actors.

As the function of the system is being detailed, it is important to also capture the expected qualities of the system. Guidance for this can be found in the task, Identify Non-Functional Requirements.

Understanding the solution requirements from the perspective of the critical users and stakeholders is crucial. A Customer Workshop (see Consultative Technical Selling Via Discovery – PoV – Customer Workshop ) can be an effective way to take one or more business scenarios mapped out in a PoV and develop an agreed upon set of Hills along with potential user/system interactions via one or more prototypes.
If the customer has settled on a particular [set of] technologies to be delivered, specialized Customer Workshops, can be an effective tool to establish concrete business and technology outcome goals (potentially within a strategic roadmap) as well as a means to capture detailed functional and non-functional requirements.
Requirements will continue to be elaborated during the Develop activity. The approach should be to establish high-level requirements during Explore and then develop requirements specific to the solution details as those architecture decisions are made.
Initial Solution Outline and Approach – Provide the first conceptual vision of the architecture as a means for playback to the key stakeholders. What architectural approach(es) will be used documenting alternatives considered and main justification for approaches selected. Further dive into critical systems (data sources) that will be influenced by the proposed system to better understand implied constraints. Many solutions will be heavily dependent on internal and/or external data. It is important to understand the nature of the data sources (where are they, how can it be accessed, what are its properties, …). Guidance supporting this investigation can be found in the task,Identify High Level Data Sources.
For communication, it will be necessary to create one or more sketches of the proposed solution in a format that resonates with the corresponding stakeholder. Guidance can be found in the task, Sketch Initial Architecture Overview.
Once you start to detail out solution elements, it is necessary to document alternatives considered and the decisions made. See the very important guidance found in the task, Document Architectural Decisions. 		The most visible output of meeting this objective is one or more initial Architecture Overview Diagrams (AODs). Frequently at this point of Exploration, you will be exploiting various assets (guidance referenced in the Assess Potential for Success objective), such as Reference Architectures. Whether the focus on these architectural assets (accelerators) are used to seed your solution or are primarily used to validate and assess the potential for success they will provide significant value. Consider consulting Solution Design accelerator tools like Solution Seeker early in the process.
Assess Potential for Success – The activity milestone is a qualified opportunity. Do we understand the scope of the project timeline, costs, need skills and resources, etc. Are we still positioned to meet the customer's needs and deliver significant value?

As noted in the Tips and Considerations above, there are many solution design accelerators available for consultation. These can be used to either seed your solution design or help in assessing the potential for success for the evolving solution design. Excellent guidance and pointers to critical resources can be found in the task, Survey Candidate Assets and Optimize Solution Approach. Within this task there is a reference to the guidance paper, Source of Candidate Assets for Team Solution Design Users.

There will be many constraints and potential risks to success that will emerge as you outline a proposed solution design. It is critical that these be documented and be front and center as you proceed. In particular, Risks to project success may lead to identifying alternative approaches to deliver similar capabilities in a way that are better matched to the client and delivery teams’ environments and ability. Guidance for how to manage an ongoing assessment of the proposed project can be found in the task,Conduct Viability Assessment .

Repeatable solutions benefit both the customer and IBM. Such opportunities will have lower risk, faster time to delivery and potentially lower costs.
When you survey candidate assets, you will also be identifying individual or organizational expertise that might be called upon to help move the current opportunity forward.
Although called out in the Understand activity, considering a Roadmap strategy may be revisited as a Solution starts to unfold. Is there a way to deliver a proposed solution in stages, delivering incremental value, with perhaps a more enticing timeline and incremental cost for the customer? This is addressed within the discussion of Solution Optimization in the task, Survey Candidate Assets and Optimize Solution Approach.

Key Considerations

One should be aware of the buyer behavior of the client and consider its impact on the approach and effort taken during the explore activity. The client buyer behaviors are:

1. Value for Money: Clients want a specific technology or service from a trusted brand configured and delivered to their specifications on time at competitive prices, simply and efficiently.
2. Trusted Supplier: Clients want innovative, best in class technology and support services, integrated with their existing infrastructure, delivered with high-touch project management from one client-focused team from the sale through delivery fulfillment.
3. Innovation Partner: Clients want a customized approach for a complex project with high strategic business impact and risk from business-oriented thought leaders and industry experts with a deep relationship-based understanding of the client.
